CaptionAI Case Study

Project Overview

CaptionAI is an AI powered caption generator that improves the workflow of Cloud Campaign customers which range from large agencies to freelancers. The product has two forms, one as a standalone product and two as an integrated feature within Cloud Campaign's agency-friendly social media management platform. Given the two forms, the purpose, the considerations, and the pain points varied from the beginning. For this case study, I will be discussing the integrated form.

Teams

Role

Sole UX/UI Designer

Key Points of Consideration

  • How to integrate and introduce the implementation without disrupting the existing work flows of our users

CaptionAI Integration or CaptionAI Product

When determining whether the integration or the separate product should take priority, we focused on where the immediate value would be added. This value being furthering our customers in their processes and the value in keeping the business up to date and relevant when it comes to AI. Taking into consideration aforementioned values, we decided to move forward with the integration first. To note, the separate product's value was in giving new and current customers an opportunity to test out CaptionAI and in providing leads for our sales team.

Research

The bulk of the research for this project was sourced from interviews between the product manager and the customers of the company. I was a part of several interviews as well where we held discussions to gather the needs and wants of our users. The pool of customers that we had access to was limited, however, efforts were made to diversify and include customers of various sizes and industries.
We focused on customers' existing workflows and what they expected from AI in general then specifically, a caption generator. We gathered information about workarounds that customers had developed. Before thinking of solutions for the captionai project, we thought about the possibility of addressing the issues causing the need for workarounds. Can we take this time to address any of these issues since we will be adapting code in this area during the implementation? Do we have enough resources with regards to engineers and time to do so?

Ideation

For this project, I carried out a design sprint to the best of my abilities in order to gather more insight and heighten collaboration. As the only designer, there was often a need for initiating contact for brainstorming and ideating. I would like to note, this was my first time running a design sprint and given all that could have gone better, it truly was better than not having one.

As previously mentioned, we were limited in our research size pool. This is where the design sprint helped gather more information. Collaborating with internal team members, who have been in consistent contact with our customers, provided key actionable insights. Many suggestions were made and of those, must haves were applied and nice to haves were documented for future updates.

Visual Design

For visual design, when creating the wireframes, I stayed consistent with the established brand color scheme. As for established patterns, decisions were made on a case by case basis. Should we keep the existing pattern or should we introduce a new one and implement backwards? I value consistency, but I am an advocate for new patterns when and where necessary, given they provide a smoother and more inclusive experience. This is also taking into consideration the time and resources available to implement the changes that follow a new pattern.
Given the pace in which projects were carried out, I often sketched designs on pencil and paper and moved them to Figma with the visual components in mind. I also wanted to provide a clearer picture to the internal team and diffuse the unfamiliarity with customers during usability testing earlier within the process. With that in mind, I recreated the existing platform on Figma as well.

Iterations

In this section, I would like to highlight a few areas of where and how iterative design took place.

At the beginning, there was a decision to make regarding where the caption generator would be implemented. The most logical and reasonable location was within areas where captions were being manually written. There are three locations for caption creation, the main Create Content Modal, the Post Now section, and the Create Content Modal within the Calendar view of the platform. The following designs are from the main Create Content Modal. The next decision from there was where within the modal.

The first option presented was the vertical scroll version shown below. This design placed the integration below the existing caption input box. The button for the feature stood inline with the emoticon, multiplatform, and hashtag options.

The second option was the horizontal navigation version shown below. This option provided a more visible button that would then take the user to a new screen within the modal upon interaction. The new screen would move in from the right and a breadcrumb would signify the movement to the users while allowing them to baivgate back to the main caption screen from the CaptionAI screen.

Cloud Campaign provides a feature in which users are able to create captions specifically for the type of social media post. When implementing CaptionAI, we debated between the following two versions. The first version shows all platforms in caption edit mode even if the platform was not selected. The second version shows only the selected platforms. We pushed forward with the second version as A/B preference test results reflected less confusion and higher learnablity. Often times, less is more.

Result and Reflection

This new feature has been successfully released and is being utilized by a noticeable majority of Cloud Campaign customers. With that, there has been an inflow of postive reviews with regards to the design and the functionality of the feature. Personally, this is one of my proudest projects at Cloud Campaign as I stepped out of my comfort zone and reached out for increased collaboration. This in turn led to a fuller list of design requirements, better understanding of the technical feasability, and clearer insight into user needs. This allowed for easier navigation when faced with scope creep, new information, and necessary adjustments.
